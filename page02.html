When your disease is hard to name and doesn’t have visible symptoms, it can be hard for others to understand that you are sick. And, when people don’t know much about your disease, it can be hard to explain it to family and friends.This sentiment is particularly true for the some 50 million people in the United States living with autoimmune diseases.

Autoimmune Disease happens when the body’s natural defense system can’t tell the difference between your own cells and foreign cells, causing the body to mistakenly attack normal cells. There are more than 80 types of autoimmune diseases that affect a wide range of body parts.

With the truth of the amount of women diagonsed, it is also the truth that women are also overlooked in the process of conducting investigations into the disease.

Miniorities such as African Americans and Latinos make up 90% of cases of SLE (Lupus) and 60% of Sclerodoma- one of the most glaring disparities in medicine. It does not help that socioeconomic determinants contribute to worsening of this condition!

There is especially a stigma against certain disorders such as the apperance of psoriasis and high divorce rates among those with multiple sclerosis and SLE (lupus). Reports suggest that within five years of the diagnosis of lupus, nearly half of the marriages have ended in divorce.

Half of patients with auto-immune conditions experience mental health conditions such as depression or anxiety, yet the majority are rarely or never asked in clinic about mental health symptoms.
